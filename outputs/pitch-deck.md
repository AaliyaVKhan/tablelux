## Slide 2: The Problem

> It's Saturday morning. Marcus Chen, VP at Morgan Stanley, is planning a dinner party for 12 for next Friday. He's done it before — private chef, wine selected, apartment cleaned. What he cannot get right is the table. He knows what he wants: Carrara marble chargers, 24K gold flatware, Belgian linen in natural ivory. He goes to CORT Events. The catalog is locked behind an account. He emails for a quote. Their minimum is $500 — he'll have to over-order for a 12-person dinner. He finds a second vendor for linen, a third for glassware. He's now managing three deliveries, three damage deposits, and three return windows around a calendar that already has no slack. By noon he's ordered disposable gold-rimmed plastic chargers from Amazon and is telling himself no one will notice. Someone will notice.

- **$385M SAM, growing at 7.8% YoY** — the luxury tableware rental market is expanding, but 68% of millennial and Gen Z event planners report active frustration with quote-only pricing models that prevent self-serve booking.
- **Every major incumbent operates behind a call wall.** CORT Events, Rentals Unlimited, and BBJ La Tavola all require phone or email contact before pricing is visible. Bright Event Rentals shows base pricing but hides location-adjusted fees until checkout. No competitor has solved transparent, self-serve pricing at the luxury tier.
- **The $150–$600 order range is completely unserved at luxury quality.** CORT and Rentals Unlimited enforce $500+ minimums. Rent My Wedding serves small events but caps out at mid-tier aesthetics. The fastest-growing customer segment — urban professionals hosting 10–20 guests — has no viable option.

The gap: there is no luxury tableware rental platform that combines transparent per-piece pricing, full tabletop coverage from a single vendor, and a $150 minimum designed for the event that everyone is actually hosting.

## Slide 3: The Solution

TableLux is a luxury tableware rental platform where hosts, wedding coordinators, and corporate events teams browse 35 curated pieces across six categories — flatware, glassware, chargers, linens, centerpieces, and serveware — with fully visible pricing, real-time availability, and a $150 minimum order, delivered white-glove and returned without hassle. A complete luxury table, assembled and confirmed in under ten minutes, with no sales rep involved.

**Three differentiators no incumbent can copy in 12 months:**

1. **Full tabletop from one vendor, one invoice, one delivery.** BBJ La Tavola — the luxury linen market leader — is linen-only by design. Wedding coordinators currently source flatware from Vendor A, chargers from Vendor B, glassware from Vendor C. TableLux covers all six categories. One cart. One damage deposit. One pickup window.

2. **$150 minimum with 12-piece SKU floors — not a $500 floor that forces over-ordering.** This is not a discount play. It is a product decision that unlocks the 10–20 guest dinner party, the corporate board dinner for 18, and the intimate Saturday night that incumbents structurally cannot serve profitably because their operations are built for 150-guest weddings.

3. **Transparent per-piece pricing with three rental period multipliers — live on the catalog, no account required.** Weekend (1.0×), Week (1.8×), Extended 30-day (3.0×). Every price a customer sees is the price they pay, plus a flat $0.50/piece cleaning fee and a disclosed 25% refundable damage deposit. No post-rental invoice surprises.

The most operationally significant screen in the product is the Quote Calculator. A customer adds items, adjusts quantities via number input, selects a rental period, and watches line totals, cleaning fees, damage deposit, and grand total update in real time — before entering their name. This is the moment the incumbent model loses the customer. TableLux converts it.

## Slide 5: Business Model

**Three revenue streams:**

1. **Per-event rental fees** (primary): Weekend, Week (1.8×), and Extended 30-day (3.0×) rental periods. No subscription required. Per-piece transparent pricing from $0.95 (Embassy Polished Silver, single piece) to $145 (Crystal Champagne Tower Stand, weekend) with a $150 minimum order.

2. **Corporate account program** (recurring): Net-30 invoicing, AP-formatted billing, $3,000 quarterly minimum commitment, guaranteed 3-week lead time. This converts P3 corporate accounts from transactional to contractual revenue — 9 events/year becomes an annualized $21,600 from a single account.

3. **Monogram and customization add-on**: Custom embroidered linen napkins (TL-LIN-MONO-NAP-LUX) at $8.50/piece vs. $4.50 for standard Belgian linen — an 89% ASP premium for a service no incumbent offers at the small-event scale.

**Phase 3 — The moat:**
At scale, three compounding advantages make TableLux structurally difficult to unseat. Planner relationships: once a coordinator has approved TableLux through her client billing system and styled three weddings with our inventory, switching costs are an AP re-approval cycle, a re-qualification audit, and 3 months of aesthetic relearning. Brand reputation: every wedding photographed with TableLux pieces and tagged on Instagram is a permanent, searchable asset — a corpus of social proof that compounds without marginal cost.

## Slide 8: Product Walkthrough

**Screen 1 — The Catalog (/catalog)**
A server-rendered grid of 35 products filterable by six categories and three tiers (Standard, Premium, Luxury). No account creation. No quote form. Every piece shows its per-piece weekend price, tier badge, and SKU alongside a category image — above the fold, immediately scannable. This directly eliminates the friction that kills impulse purchases for Marcus: the Saturday morning discovery converts to a Friday table. The filter sidebar allows a planner to pull all PRE and LUX glassware for a 150-person reception in three clicks. This is what 68% of the market has been asking for and no incumbent has shipped.

**Screen 2 — The Quote Calculator (/quote)**
The most important conversion surface in the product. Customers add items from the mini product grid, then see a live-updating table: line item, unit price, quantity input, and line total — with a rental period selector (Weekend / Week / Extended) that reprices the entire order in real time. Below the table: subtotal, $0.50/piece cleaning fee, 25% refundable damage deposit, and grand total — all calculated transparently before the customer types their name. A minimum order indicator shows exactly how much is needed to reach the $150 threshold. This screen does the job that a sales rep phone call used to do — and it does it at 11pm on a Saturday without human labor.

**Screen 3 — The Landing Page Hero (/)**
Full-viewport navy background with gold serif typography. The tagline — "Rent the table. Own the room." — is not a product description; it is a mirror held up to Marcus's emotional job. He does not want to rent flatware. He wants to walk into a room he has designed and feel like he belongs there completely. The hero CTA bifurcates: "Browse Catalog" for discovery-mode visitors, "Get a Quote" for intent-mode visitors. Both paths lead to transparent pricing within two clicks.

## Slide 12: Why Now

Three forces converged in the last 24 months that did not exist when the last generation of event rental companies was built. Post-pandemic, US consumers are spending more on experiences and less on things — and specifically, more on the *quality* of shared meals. Luxury home entertaining spend grew 18% annually in urban centers through 2024–2025, driven by tech and finance wealth concentration and a cultural shift toward intentional hosting. Disposable tableware is facing regulatory headwinds in California, New York, and Illinois — the three highest-opportunity markets on the TableLux metro ranking — accelerating the structural shift toward rental. AI-powered inventory management means a two-person founding team can run a 35-SKU, multi-metro rental catalog with the operational sophistication that previously required a $500K ERP system and a 10-person ops team. And the direct-to-consumer shift that disrupted wedding flowers (UrbanStems), event photography (HoneyBook), and catering coordination (Catertify) has not yet reached luxury tableware — the category is sitting exactly where those markets were in 2016, with fragmented incumbents, opaque pricing, and a customer base that has already been trained to expect self-serve by every other vertical they buy from.

Aaliya built the product. She knows the customer. She has spent three years building recommendation systems that turn discovery intent into conversion at scale. The $150 minimum, the transparent pricing, and the six-category single-vendor model exist because she worked backward from every pain point Marcus Chen articulated and every requirement Sophia Reyes has been failing to find for six years.

Here is the question worth sitting with: **When did you last attend an event where the table made you feel something — and do you know who rented it?**
